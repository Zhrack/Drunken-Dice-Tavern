            Design a Dungeon

            Design a Dungeon

            Jan 21, 2024, 21 min read

            What is needed:

            • Dungeon Map
            • Dungeon Key

            Recipe: 5 + 5 Dungeons ยง

            This is an adventure recipe for a simple dungeon adventure. Just add the ingredients to the skeleton! This should be enough for a 4 hours session of play.

            1: Scenario Concept ยง

            Start a new empty note and follow this section: 1 Brainstorming. To define the concept: Location, Denizens and Goal.

            2: Dungeon Features & Corridor Themes ยง

            In another blank note, put the keys of the dungeon, starting with General Dungeon Features and Corridor Themes.

            3: Five Featured Rooms ยง

            Create five featured rooms:

            1. 1 Challenge Room
            2. 1 Fight Room
            3. 1 Twist Room
            4. 1 Reward Room
            5. Another of Challenge, Fight, Twist or Reward room.

            4: Scenic Rooms ยง

            Now, create five scenic rooms. Follow Scenic Room.

            5: Mapping & Keying ยง

            Follow 3 Mapping and 4 Keying. Add navigational choices as well, like

            • Forks in corridors
            • Rooms with more than 1 exit
            • Secret doors and passages
            • Crossroads

            6: Finishing Touches ยง

            • Revisit 2 Dungeon Features & Corridor Themes
            • Clean up notes and add details as needed.
            • Design a Scenario Hook for PCs. Having everything defined, there should be plenty of inspiration to choose from.
              • Classic hooks:
                • A patron hires them to go to the dungeon and do something
                • PCs found a treasure map or rumors talking about the dungeon
                • PCs were attacked by someone from the dungeon
              • Check more hooks on page 498

            Challenge Room ยง

            These are dilemmas, a tough choice between two options. Different types (roll 1d5):

            Trap ยง

            Follow Running Traps.

            Environmental ยง

            Can be a hazard like a trap, but also other challenge. They evoke the dungeon location, making it influence the gameplay instead of only being scenery.

            • Broken bridge over a chasm
            • Force fields blocking a hallway
            • Debilitating heat that exhausts over time unless measures are taken
            • Polluted river that needs to be cleaned
            • Cliff to climb
            • Needing shelter during sudden blizzard
            • Swimming across rapids filled river
            • Navigating through an area of magical darkness
            • A drawbridge that needs to be lowered to access the castle.

            Skill ยง

            Thinking of a skill check-first challenge. Itโs important to give the player also a way to figure out the solution (OSR style) before doing the check.

            Tip

            Present a situation where the player needs to find a solution, THEN ask for the skill check. Bonus points if the players can make their own solution or if they require multiple steps to resolve.

            Puzzle ยง

            Should always be optional, because they are fragile if players donโt have the correct insight. If they are not blocking optional content, add alternatives to continue.

            Different types

            • Riddle
            • Encoded message
            • Set of colored objects to manipulate in a certain way
            • A maze
            • Lock with combination to be found
            • Etc

            Note

            IMO, could be interesting for in person play, less interesting online

            Social ยง

            Can be an alternative to a Fight room as well, there is overlap.

            Examples

            • Bribing for info or service
            • Persuading someone or some faction of a fact or to do something
            • Being asked to do something morally questionable as a shortcut to a solution
            • Interrogation
            • Adjudicating a feud between two NPCs

            Fight Room ยง

            Check Combat Encounter Design.

            Different types (roll 1d4 but should focus on the first 3)

            Guardian ยง

            Protecting something o someone, maybe just itself. Most classic.

            Sentry ยง

            A sentry will attempt to raise the alarm somehow. Common variant: a part fights, the rest raises the alarm. Should be kept a simpler encounter, since it can attract nearby rooms.

            Think about possible consequence if alarm is raised.

            Mooks ยง

            Should be on the easy side to give a change of pace and making PCs be badasses. They can be reinforcements for a Sentry encounter as well.

            Boss ยง

            Should be hard. Should feature additional mooks as well for ablative shielding.

            Twist Room ยง

            Complication, surprise, inversion of expectations. (roll 1d5)

            Trick ยง

            Appears to be one thing, but itโs actually another. Examples:

            • Chained maiden is actually a medusa waiting to strike
            • Fake treasure as trap
            • A collaborating servant is actually an important NPC
            • Illusion If the entire dungeon has some misdirection, this room is where they discover the truth.

            Secret Area ยง

            Bonus area, shortcut to another place or for tactical advantage.

            Remix ยง

            Take on of the other rooms in the dungeon and twist it or invert it, letting the players use their experience with the other chamber. If the other is a Scenic room, maybe here there is something hidden. If itโs a challenge, an alternative solution is required. If itโs a fight, terrain is different or variant enemies. Etcโฆ

            Combo ยง

            Take two different room archetypes and combine them. Boss is a puzzle as well? Or fight encounter has a trap that is used by the enemies? Etcโฆ

            Unexpected Ending ยง

            Revelation room where they think of having finished, but actually the solution is still missing. Can be a plot twist or simply a clue that the dungeonโs goal doesnโt end in this dungeon but in another adventure.

            Reward Room ยง

            Should be mechanical and/or narrative. (roll 1d5)

            Treasure ยง

            Items, money, jewels, weapons, armors, rare books, trade goods, etc. Having specific treasure can introduce logistic problems (how do you move the loot out of the dungeon? Need a clever solution). Liquidating a treasure can also be hooks for more adventures.

            Clue ยง

            Vital piece of information about another room or a different adventure.

            Sanctuary ยง

            Place of safety to rest and respite inside the dungeon. If used, might trigger a restocking or could be a fake sanctuary.

            Boon ยง

            There is a benefit or advantage for PCs. Should be aligned to the dungeon theme.

            Scenario Goal ยง

            Here is the goal of the dungeon. Often also a Combo Twist room, with a Boss or Guardian fight, a trap or similar.

            Scenic Room ยง

            Scenic rooms are not interactive and are useful to multiple things:

            • Reveal world or location lore
            • Foreshadowing of nearby featured room
            • Cool description to create atmosphere
            • Some Trick (Tome of Adv Design)

            Can be split in 3 types:

            Themed Scenic Room ยง

            They establish, develop or manipulate the themes of the dungeon. The themes are about the place, a character, concept, ideology, mood, composition.

            Look at the dungeon concept, how can this themed room reflect the location, denizens or goal?

            Example

            For a bandit camp with trained wolves, a themed room can be the empty kennels, foreshadowing the wolves in another featured room.

            Utility Scenic Room ยง

            These are places that should logically exist given the concept of the dungeon. They can be the backdrop of featured rooms, but can be scenic rooms as well.

            Example

            • A dwarven city should have forges.
            • A bandit camp should have barracks for sleeping.

            Fantastic Feature Scenic Room ยง

            Use the impossible and put it here.

            Example

            • A levitation sculpture formed from floating amethysts
            • Magical painting that come to life
            • Tree with metallic leaves
            • Taxidermied unicorn
            • Waterfall of black acid
            • Room of magical portals leading to other rooms
            • Observatory showing the sky of another place
            • A shrine to unknown gods

            They can also be non-fantastical, but still exotic and awe-inspiring. They are an opportunity to highlight your chosen genre.

            Dungeon Types ยง

            Micro-dungeon ยง

            Very small, 1-2 featured rooms and 1-2 scenic rooms. Usually a total of 5 rooms. Can be easily incorporated in another adventure for a single scene almost.

            Large Dungeons ยง

            Usually at least three times a 5 + 5 Dungeon. Requires multiple expeditions to explore, which changes the approach and pace of play.

            Some advices:

            • Needs different goal than โClear the dungeonโ to give strategic choice while exploring.
            • Xandered dungeon design.
            • Mix experiences in the dungeon. Use thematic areas to divide it and change pace, different encounter checks and random encounter tables if possible.
            • Make the dungeon come to life with random encounters (or, to make it more dynamic, Adversary Rosters), restocking and updating the dungeon key while playing.

            These and Mega Dungeons can be split in several levels. Maybe each level is its own 5 + 5 Dungeon? Connectors connect them vertically:

            • Stairs of various types (stright, spiral, helical etc)
            • Slopes
            • Chutes
            • Ladders
            • Trapdoors
            • Windows
            • Teleports
            • Traps
            • Multi-level chambers
            • Elevators
            • Basket & Pulleys
            • Rivers
            • Underwater
            • Collapsed passages

            Mega dungeons ยง

            Bigger than a Large Dungeon, has enough content for a full campaign. Contains multiple scenarios, like multiple goals and/or multiple factions. Factions control chunks of the dungeon and give unique challenges.

            Starting a Megadungeon ยง

            Prepping the first 3 levels should be enough, then after the first session, check how many rooms were explored and use that as an estimate for where they could reach next time at the worst case scenario.

            In case you want to make a basic plan of the entire megadungeon, a solution might be to list the major factions in each level to have inspiration for faction relations.

            Jaquaysed dungeons ยง

            Aka non-linear structure of dungeons. Useful techniques:

            • Loops!
              • Add landmarks to provide indications to players.
            • Multiple entrances
            • Multiple level connections
            • Discontinuous level connections
              • Connections skipping levels. Useful also when returning and skipping ahead to lower levels.
            • Secret & unusual paths
            • Sub-Levels
              • Smaller section of a level
            • Divided levels
              • Level is split in multiple parts disconnected from each other. Need to go to other levels to reach.
            • Minor elevation shifts
            • Midpoint entry (of vertical levels)

            General procedure:

            1: Brainstorming ยง

            To define:

            1. Scenario concept, formed by:
              1. Location
              2. Denizens
                1. Can be decided from a monster list as well
                2. Pick two types of denizens and mix them
                  1. Why are they together?
              3. Goal
                1. Maybe a MacGuffin?

            Itโs good to start from one of the three elements and go from there.

            Possible locations examples

            1d20Location type
            1Cave
            2Ruins
            3Castleโs oubliette
            4Subterranean city
            5Crypt or catacombs
            6Mine
            7Mansion
            8Pyramid
            9Tower
            10Ship or shipwreck
            11Colossal body
            12City building or businness
            13Security vault
            14Stronghold or fortress
            15Cliff dwellings
            16Treetop buildings
            17Subacquatic complex
            18Pocket dimension
            19Cloud cities
            20Prison

            2: List rooms & scenes ideas ยง

            Based on the concept, make a list of cool location, needed locations for the general location to make sense and also possible cool scenes or obstacles that can be present.

            A bullet list with a short phrase for each is enough for now. No need to list them all, new ideas will come as well.

            Questions to answer: ยง

            • What challenges need to be overcome to achieve the goal?
            • What logically needs to exist in this location?
            • What cool things can the denizens do, and what environments would make that even more fun?
            • What are some common features of a location like this? (Castle โ Throne room)
            • What fantastical twist could you apply to common rooms?
            • How might the special abilities of NPCs create unique needs or alter the functions of common rooms?

            Some of these elements can be merged together to have something better!

            3: Mapping ยง

            Make a rough graph of how rooms are connected together. New ideas coming up should be added to the rooms list.

            After some iterations on it, you will have a good graph, which can be converted to a detailed map. While making the detailed map, number the rooms. If having multiple levels, vertical connections like stairs should have labels indicating the room key they connect to in the destination level, for ease of use.

            If making a map with Dungeon Alchemist, I can put the exported image in Excalidraw and add numbers on it there.

            4: Keying ยง

            General Dungeon Features ยง

            At the beginning, decide about common things to all or most rooms and make a description. For big or mega dungeons, these could change by level or by other type of cluster of rooms.

            Usual stuff in a brief bullet-point list:

            1. Illumination
            2. Ceiling height
            3. Walls
            4. Doors

            Corridor Themes ยง

            Same as for General Dungeon Features section. Having a few general elements that can be present and decide randomly what each corridor has is a good idea.

            Room Keys ยง

            For each room:

            • Name: Quick remainder of content and purpose
            • Boxed Text: Initial description of room, follow Exits & Dimensions.
            • Reactive Skill Checks: Descriptions in response to likely skill checks
            • Room Elements: Detailed description of the room content.
            • Type: Featured or Scenic? (Reminder for me to have a good balance during design)

            Featured rooms are where most of the action and interaction is. They can be of different types, like in 3 Five Featured Rooms. Add at least 3 interactive elements to each featured room. Itโs important to have each PC do something during the same Dungeon Turn.

            Rules to make Awesome Descriptions ยง

            Three of Five ยง

            Of the five senses, include three of them in the description.

            Two Cool Details ยง

            Add a couple of irrelevant but cool details in descriptions. Might be useful to players, but they are especially useful in creating atmosphere.

            Three-by-Three ยง

            Each of the three interactive elements in featured rooms should have three description details.

            Example

            One interactive element: A bookshelf โ A tall bookshelf in the corner, its shelves crammed with scrolls and books.

            Exits & Dimensions ยง

            At the beginning of entering a room, prioritize this order of descriptions:

            1. Size and shape of the room
              1. Can be precise (20x30 ft) or general (โLarge chamber, โsmall oval room"")
              2. Donโt forget height!
            2. Exits from the room.
            3. Describe the room. Use Rules to make Awesome Descriptions for help.
            4. Describe the Room Elements with Three of Five and Three-by-Three

            5: Combat Encounter Design ยง

            After keying rooms, you will have some monsters/NPCs.

            1. Check their abilities/stat block. What terrain feature can be useful to those abilities?
            2. Then, try to have different monsters for variety. Also variants of the same monster as well.

            Random Encounter Table ยง

            Time to also fill a random encounter table.

            Choose a size. 2d6 is a general good rule, with more common elements in the middle. The encounters can be bespoke or procedural, but both types should be either easy or medium encounters, because they could be combined with nearby encounters. Check also Adversary Rosters if you want more control of dynamic groups of denizens doing specific stuff. Also Dungeon Factions to have factions politics involved.

            Bespoke Encounters ยง

            Aka prepped encounter. They are specific creatures doing specific, predesigned things. A setpiece.

            Example

            5 orcs are heading to the archery range, boasting about how well they will shoot. Two goblins bring their equipment. The group make opposing perception and stealth checks against PCs to see who sees what.

            Procedurally Generated Encounters ยง

            They are kernels for situations, to be improvised on the spot based on local situation and inspiration. The Reaction check and Distance Check can help in giving inspiration.

            There can be also a specific NPC in the random table, no need to limit to generic creatures.

            Idea

            My idea: To help with on-the-fly improv, together with the creatures, each row could have pre-rolled oracles on Why they are there, Where they are going, Where they come from (which area of the dungeon or outside).

            Steps to build it:

            1. Choose or roll 1 or more creature types
            2. How many for an easy or medium encounter?
            3. Dice range to randomize exact quantities

            One of the rows could be โRoll again twiceโ to have combined encounters.

            Where do they come from? ยง

            • Option 1: Remove them from around the Dungeon Key
            • Option 2: Have a pool of wandering monsters from which to get them. A pool for each type of creature.
            • Option 3: Not tracked at all, random encounters always available. I prefer either 2 or 3. 2 is good for smaller dungeons.

            On-the-Fly Encounters (for Solo) ยง

            Example of Encounters for on-the-fly exploration, like in Ironsworn Delve. From https://chaoclypse.substack.com/p/soloing-dungeons-the-osr-way

            Encounter List (d10)

            1d10Result
            1-4
            5-6
            7-8
            9
            10Random Encounter
            On rolling a 10, roll for a random encounter based on the dungeon level you are on.

            On rolling on an empty entry, roll for a random encounter or generate a creature and fill it in.

            You may notice that the encounter list is staggered in terms of probability. This makes it so that there is a โdominantโ faction on each dungeon level. Feel free to adjust this, or make the chances equal.

            This way, over time you will get more familiar with what lives in the dungeon โ factions naturally emerge. However, you can still be completely taken off guard, especially when you roll a 10!

            Restocking ยง

            In smaller or regular dungeons (like 5+5 Dungeons), you will probably need a new scenario concept as well. In bigger dungeons, restocking can also be part of its story, that now is influenced by the PCsโ previous actions. Since they are making multiple expeditions, the situation will evolve accordingly. In mega dungeons restocking is part of the evolving narrative.

            Procedure ยง

            1. Divide the dungeon in zones. They can be:
              1. A single 5+5 Dungeon segment (if they are the dungeon building blocks)
              2. A level (can be further divided in smaller zones)
              3. Chokepoints
              4. Lairs of factions, which would logically still attract other factions for some logistical or military reason
            2. Make a Restocking Check (1 on 1d8) (doable also during long rests inside the dungeon) by choosing the zones to test. They can be:
              1. Empty/Cleared Zones
              2. Disrupted Zones: Where PCs passed through.
              3. Everything: Doesnโt make sense to reroll prepped and not used stuff. Do not use.
            3. If 1, either do a Custom Restock or a Procedural Restock.
              1. Custom Restock: Based on the story so far, develop what the affected factions and creatures will do and restock based on that. For example, they could ask help to other levels/factions/outside. This will prompt you to also make new factions as well, helping worldbuilding.
              2. Procedural Restock:
                1. Roll on a Stocking Table (like a random encounter table) to determine which creatures are coming. (The random encounter table can be reused as well)
                2. Add minor treasure to the zone, carried by the new denizens. On a 1 on 1d8, add a major treasure as well.

            Updating the dungeon key ยง

            Usually done while restocking. While playing, note main actions that affect a room made by the PCs. After the session, if there are enough changes, make a Scenario Update, a new note working like a diff file. Itโs a list of changes that were made by PCs. Keep it simple and minimal, updating the original Room Key entry to reflect those changes. When accumulating these Updates, creating a new Campaign Status Document. Another approach is to create a new Dungeon Key increment version, in case the diff has become too big. Just copy the old one and update where needed.

            Idea

            I would personally have a small section under each room key for the small updates. If the changes are too much, just copy that individual room keys that are too big and update the description, to avoid having to do a big chunk of work.

            Dynamic Dungeons ยง

            Additional techniques to have dungeons respond to PCsโ actions.

            Adversary Rosters ยง

            A tool to detach NPCs and monsters from rooms and having them doing things around. Useful mainly in regular and smaller dungeons, for bigger ones, random encounters are good enough and easier to keep track of.

            The Roster is a list of denizens separate from the room key, where they donโt appear.

            Example

            Action GroupAreaNotes
            4 mud mephitsArea 1
            2 soke mephits + 1 steam mephitArea 2
            Work Crew: 2 magmin + 2 mud mephitsArea 8Moving between A8 & A10

            The denizens can be busy with patrols, working between 2 points, going from A to B, etcโฆ

            The basic chunk is the Action Group to group together denizens that do something together. At the start, you can have more AG in the same spot, before splitting doing their thing. They should also have stat blocks if needed, for quick reference. Still ticking time, but no Random Encounter checks.

            Action Group Types ยง

            Patrols ยง

            Make a looping circuit through a location. Can be useful to have a separate note for Patrols if there are many, with the route signed.

            Mobile ยง

            Default group. Keyed to a location but can respond to external stimuli.

            Mostly Stationary ยง

            Unlikely to leave the area. Could be a choice or not. If they could become Mobile, they should be an AG. Signal them in the Roster to quickly ignore them, like indenting the entry.

            Stationary ยง

            Will never leave the location they are in. Not included in the roster, but in the room key instead.

            Advanced Rosters ยง

            Variable Areas ยง

            Some AGs could move between areas instead or remaining in the specific group of rooms. On the Roster list them.

            Example

            Few examples

            • Area 21 or Area 40.
            • Area 21 (1-2), Area 40 (4-6) on 1d6
            • Area 21 (day), Area 40 (night)

            Do not go too granular, details should come from play.

            Notes/Footnotes ยง

            Adding notes to the AGs is useful for adding specific details to the AG, have tactical notes, if Mostly Stationary, why? and other things to keep in mind or to speed up generation (Names?)

            Multiple Rosters ยง

            Could be useful in case of particular scenarios. Day Roster and Night Roster? Normal status and Alert status? Only useful if there is a big change for some reason, otherwise having small conditionals in individual AGs is enough.

            Split Rosters ยง

            For large dungeons, having multiple Rosters simplifies management. One Roster per level, for a megadungeon for example, or some special role for AGs, like VIPs or active response teams.

            Rosters & Random Encounters ยง

            Usually one or the other. Rosters for smaller locations, REs for bigger ones. Hybrid approach is possible by continue checking for REs while playing. One example is in a megadungeon, a Roster for each level, but RE checks for eventual denizens of other levels passing through. Another example is to randomize Patrols and keep the other more passive types as AGs.

            Roster Restocking ยง

            Restocking is faster, just create a new one for the disturbed areas. Minimal updates needed to room keys in this case, usually.

            Passive Locations ยง

            Rosters are useful for active dungeons. A dusty tomb undisturbed for centuries might not require one, for example.

            Dynamic Encounter Design ยง

            With dynamic dungeons, you need a dynamic way to design encounters. Having encounters be combined is common with Actions Groups moving around, so you want to keep each AG a easy encounter alone, since the difficulty goes up if multiple AGs are encountered together.

            Principles of Dynamic Encounter Design ยง

            • Avoid single boss encounters, difficult to balance and tactically uninteresting.
            • Single AG encounters still provide some resource ablation even if easy.
            • Two or three AGs should be a challenging encounter.
            • Four or more AGs should still be winnable, but should be very heavy on resources or require clever thinking. (rare occurrence and usually AGs join during battle, not all together) For narrative systems, these principles are less useful imho. At world level, you can have a summary of the faction (if present outside the dungeon) using Coterie Faction System.

            Dungeon Factions ยง

            If a dungeon is completely controlled by a single faction, itโs a Lair. Instead, more factions can be competing in the dungeon. They give also opportunities to PCs and to disrupt the current status quo. During restocking, faction dynamics can be updated to reflect current happenings.

            Creating Factions ยง

            You will need:

            • Faction roster
            • Faction territory
            • Faction agenda
            • Faction reaction

            Faction Roster ยง

            Itโs the Adversary Roster of the area controlled by this faction, with notes about alliances etc.

            Faction Territory ยง

            The area controlled. Can be useful to color code rooms belonging to the same faction to better visualize. There can also be unclaimed areas as well, actually itโs logic between factions.

            Faction Agenda ยง

            What do they want to achieve?

            Faction Reaction ยง

            Can be the default Reaction table or personalized, based on previous encounters with PCs or simple predisposition.

            Random Faction Conflict ยง

            What are the relations between factions? If uninspired, roll them. Make a Random Faction Table, listing the factions and assign a die. Add three entries:

            • Wandering Adventurers: represents other competing groups.
            • Outsiders: itโs a prompt for factions outside of the dungeon, like another nearby location.
            • Roll Again Twice: for multi-faction conflicts.

            Now follow this procedure:

            1. Faction Conflict Check: roll 1d6. 1 = conflict has broken out.
            2. Roll twice on the faction table. If same faction, could it be internal conflict?
            3. Roll on Faction Conflict Table to determine outcome
