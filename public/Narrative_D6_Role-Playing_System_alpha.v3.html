            Introduction §

            Inspirations §

            There were a few inspirations that stood out when designing the Narrative D6 Role-Playing System. Some of the top ones are listed here:

            • Sine Nomine Publishing’s Scarlet Heroes
            • EDGE Studio’s Genesys
            • EDGE Studio’s End of the World
            • Runehammer’s Index Card RPG

            In reality, ever role-playing game is an inspiration whether or not it is directly called out.

            What experience will this system deliver §

            The goal for this role-playing system is to deliver something beyond a binary yes or no, pass or fail feel while still making the game accessible to others without having to buy funky dice, print your own, or use stickers. The most will be listed in the system’s requirements.

            Using this system, you’ll still get a pass or fail, but the results will also deliver alterations to the results such as advantageous failures, or despairing successes. These kinds of results are meant to help the players as well as the game master exercise their narrative creativity and drive an emergent narrative that may change on the fly. Using some cancellation, bounded results and very little math, this system should help deliver rules to adjudicate tasks and give results. The rules won’t cover everything, but then a rule set never has and requires a bit of spirit of the law to adjudicate fairly and consistently. More on this in the Game Mastering section, which is highly recommended everyone reads. The role-play system is for everyone and being The Core Mechanic is easy, the rest helps players and Game Masters expand play.

            Requirements to play §

            The tools to play the Narrative D6 Role-Playing System, or ND6 RPS or ND6, should be fairly accessible. A friendly local game store should carry multi-polyhedral dice sets. The rest is for notes and other writings.

            • One set of standard polyhedral RPG dice (d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, d20)
            • Two sets of 4-12 d6s. One set one color for Positive Dice (PD). One set one color for Negative Dice (ND).
            • Index Cards.
            • US Letter Paper or graph paper for larger character sheets.
            • Pencils and pens to write information down.

            The thoughts with the graph paper is that some players are do-it-yourselfers who like to create their own versions of character sheets or inventory layouts or just mapping things. The index cards can be used for smaller versions of the character sheets for mobile or quick play.

            If you find that you don’t have two different sets of colored dice, but you have a permanent marker and some dice from your local convenience store, you can color one set of dice a different color. For instance, on my desk, I have one red and one green permanent marker and 10 six-sided dice from the store. I colored on set of five green, and one set of six red.

            The Core Mechanic §

            The ND6 RPG uses a d6 dice-pooling system as its Core Mechanic. Here are the steps to perform a Check for every action in the game:

            The Check §

            1. Each character has six attributes: Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma. Each attribute has a value of 1-5.
            2. The starting dice pool is made up of one Positive Die (PD) and one Negative Die (ND). This represents the character as a thinking individual.
            3. Add +1 PD for each Boon that applies to the check. Boons can be Skill Ranks, Good Quality Items, or Situational Modifiers. There is no limit to how many Boons can be provided.
            4. Add +1 ND for each Bane that applies to the check. Banes can be Difficulty Level (see Difficulty Chart), Poor Quality Items, or Situational Modifiers. There is no limit to how many Banes can be provided.
            5. Roll the dice pools separately.
            6. Cancel any matches between the Positive Dice Pool and Negative Dice Pool.
            7. Each PD that remains and is equal to or less than the character’s attribute counts as a Success. Only one Success is needed to succeed.
            8. If no PDs roll under the attribute or are left after canceling, the check is a Failure.
            9. If there are more PDs than NDs left, the character has Advantages equal to the number of PDs minus NDs. This happens regardless of Success or Failure.
            10. If there are more NDs than PDs left, the character has Threats equal to the number of NDs minus PDs. This happens regardless of Success or Failure.
            11. Any matches on remaining PD count as Triumphs. This happens regardless of Success or Failure.
            12. Two or more sixes rolled on remaining ND count as Despairs. This happens regardless of Success or Failure.

            The default dice pool consists of 1 PD and 1 ND. With this pool, players will either succeed or fail without any Advantages or Threats. To succeed, the player needs to roll at least one PD equal to or under their character’s attribute rank, which starts at 1. To fail, either the PD needs to be greater than the attribute rank, or the ND needs to cancel the PD by matching its value.

            Advantages are positive outcomes that happen regardless of success or failure. They can include discovering extra information or completing the task more efficiently. Threats are negative outcomes that happen regardless of success or failure. They can include alerting enemies or damaging equipment.

            Triumphs and Despairs are special results that combine Success or Failure with Advantages or Threats. A Triumph is a Success and an Advantage, while a Despair is a Failure and a Threat. Triumphs and Despairs do not cancel each other out.

            To increase their chances of Success or generate Advantages, players can add Boons to their character. Boons are bonuses such as skill ranks, good quality items, or situational modifiers, and they provide a single +1PD per Boon. To increase the chances of Failure or generate Threats, players can add Banes to their character. Banes are negative factors such as lacking skills or having poor quality equipment, and they provide a single +1ND per Bane. There is no limit to the size of the dice pool.

            Example Check §

            Trudy has a character named Olyssia the fighter. Olyssia’s characteristics are rated as: Strength 4, Dexterity 2, Constitution 3, Intelligence 1 Wisdom 2 and Charisma 2. Olyssia has a +1PD long sword. One of her Traits is goading duelist for a +2PD when fighting a single target with only her sword. So, this sets the Dice Pool to: 1PD (starting) + 1PD for the sword, and +2PD for the Trait. The total Positive Dice pool is 4PD.

            The target Olyssia is attempting to hit is a Moderate Difficulty for +2ND and has a Trait of Passive Fighter for +1ND against any aggressive targets. So, this sets up the Dice pool to: 1ND (starting) + 2ND for the Moderate difficulty + 1ND for Passive Duelist. This brings the Negative Dice pool to 4ND. An even split of Positive and Negative Dice.

            Trudy rolls the combined dice pool.

            • Positive Dice Pool: [1, 3, 5, 6]
            • Negative Dice Pool: [1, 2, 4, 6]

            To resolve this check, Trudy starts by canceling matches. The ones match and sixes match, so those are removed from the pool. Trudy is left with a three and a five. The three is below Olyssia’s Strength Attribute of four, making this check a success! Next, Trudy checks for any Advantages. Being that there are an even number of Positive and Negative Dice left in the Pool, there are no Advantages or Threats. There are no doubles in the remaining Positive Dice so there is no Triumph. There are not any double sixes in the Negative Dice pool either, so there is no Despair.

            Olyssia is successful in her strike and deals damage according to the weapons damage rating. This example is very simple, and mainly geared towards one-shot sessions. If doing long-term play, characters have armor, weapons, gear, skills and traits adding more dice to the dice pool and making things get more interesting!

            One-Shot Sessions §

            When using these mechanics during a one-shot session, use these recommendations:

            • Assign 16 XP between the six attributes at a cost of 1 XP per point.
            • Check out the Traits section for a bit more flavor to one-shot characters.

            These kinds of characters fit well on an index card. An example of what the index card layout can look like will be in the end of the book.

            Long-Term Play §

            If players want to play a longer running game with awards and increases, read the rest of this book for how to build a character and progress them.

            Character Building §

            Players wanting characters that will be used for a long term adventure or campaign should follow the character building steps listed in this chapter.

            1. Pick optional Hindrances (maximum of four).
            2. Assign points to Attributes (12 XP budget).
            3. Figure out Derived Statistics (Wounds, Toughness, Strain).
            4. Assign points to Skills (20 XP budget).
            5. Design Traits and assign points (3 XP budget).
            6. Pick or buy Equipment such as Armor, Weapons, Gear.

            If the player wants to create a character that can wield awesome power:

            1. Learn and design Powers for the character’s setting and genre.

            Powers are discussed later in the book.

            Hindrances §

            • A character can have only a maximum of four Hindrances.
            • Hindrances provide 1 XP to a character’s XP budget.
            • Hindrances provide mechanical effects such as +1 ND to a dice pool.

            A Hindrance is a narrative and potentially a mechanical set back to a character. These can give permission to players to role play their character in accordance to these Hindrances. Hindrances are completely optional as well. If the Game Master allows it, characters can receive one XP per Hindrance to a maximum of four. This means that a character can only have four Hindrances. Also, if a Game Master allows it, characters can receive a ND to a dice pool and in some special circumstances a PD depending on the context.

            An example of a Hindrance is “A Fear of Heights”. When the character is in a situation where they are chasing a target through a stair well and the target bursts out onto a roof top leaping from building to building, the player’s character would receive +1 ND if they attempt to follow their target across the roof tops.

            Attributes §

            • Characters have six attributes: Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom and Charisma.
            • Characters start with a budget of 12 XP.
            • Each attribute starts with a rank of 1.
            • Each attribute rank increase costs the value of the rank in XP.
            • The maximum an attribute can be raised to is five ranks.

            Attributes are a way to describe the raw, intrinsic abilities of a character. When a character attempts to do something without a skill or a skill doesn’t cover the task at hand, characters use their attributes. The rank of an attribute dictates the target number a player needs to roll in order for their character to succeed at a task. This means that if an attribute is rank 3, the player needs to roll 3 or less in order to success. Rolling 4 or more on a die means it failed in this instance. When increasing an attribute’s rank, players must spend the number of ranks in XP. For instance, to go from rank 1 to 2, that is 2 XP. To go from 2 to 3, that is 3 XP. So to go from rank 1 to 3 is 5 XP in total.

            Let’s discuss the various attributes.

            Strength §

            Strength is a measure of a character’s pure physical power. When a character would need to perform a physical feat such as lifting a heavy log, bending prison bars, or swinging a fist, this attribute comes into play.

            Dexterity §

            Dexterity is the character’s agility, finesse, and ability to flow with a punch or incoming blow. Dexterity also covers fine motor skills like using a lock pick. If a character is needing to hop from stone to stone, or balance on a rope, Dexterity is the key stat.

            Constitution §

            Constitution is a character’s ability to take a blow head on, stomach bad food, or handle being pushed around a bit. Even the biggest, strongest character could be a little daisy who cannot take a hit without Constitution.

            Intelligence §

            Intelligence is how fast a character can learn something, retain new knowledge or recall that knowledge accurately. Intelligence isn’t how smart a character is, but how fast they can learn and retain. Intelligence is highly sought after in arcane circles for casting magic or using mind powers.

            Wisdom §

            Wisdom is when a character can recall a lesson they learned through their actions that you cannot learn in books or formal learning institutions. Wisdom can be used to perform scouting actions, understand nature, or predict the actions of other beings in their universe. Essence powers are driven by this attribute.

            Charisma §

            Charisma is how comely, enigmatic, or likable a character is. It can also be used to assert a character’s will upon other beings in their universe. Being able to influence others is important to some characters such as when bartering or when lying. Holy powers are driven by this attribute.

            Derived Statistics §

            • Wounds are equal to 8 plus Constitution Ranks for a normal human.
            • Toughness is equal to 8 plus Strength Ranks for a normal human.
            • Strain is equal to 8 plus Wisdom Ranks for a normal human.

            Skills §

            • Start with a 20XP budget to assign to skills.
            • Skills start at rank zero to a maximum of five.
            • Each rank costs the five times the value of the skill rank.
              • Rank one is five XP, rank two is ten XP.
            • Each rank in a skill adds PD to a character’s check.

            Skills are the main way that characters achieve their goals in addition to other boons gained through quality gear or situational modifiers.

            Each Skill has an associated Attribute. These attributes dictate what the target number is for the skill being used.

            Skills have a rank of zero to five. When purchasing ranks in a skill, it costs five times the skill’s rank in XP. This means to go from rank zero to rank one, it costs five XP. Then to go from rank one to rank two, it costs ten XP. To go from rank zero to rank two, the cost would be 15 XP.

            Each rank in a skill adds +1PD to the character’s Positive Dice pool.

            Skill Permissions §

            A Skill or Trait is required in one of these.

            Arcane §

            Studied logic of the realm or plane.

            Divine §

            Draw in a deity’s power to yourself. Subject to the deity.

            Occult §

            Studied logic of alien philosophies and powers.

            Primal §

            Natural raw power. Drawn from nature itself.

            Schools of Powers §

            Abjuration §

            Protection, Suppression, Defensive Spells and Barriers.

            Conjuration §

            Creation of things such a portals, temporary items, or summoning fantastical entities to your location.

            Divination §

            Perceiving the future, places, and people. Also can uncover mysteries of magic.

            Enchantment §

            Manipulate creatures, people or things to your will. This does not physically alter the target.

            Evocation §

            Destruction, blasts, and explosions.

            Illusion §

            Creation of visual, auditory, or physical effects that trick the target.

            Necromancy §

            Two schools of thought. As both deal with life and death, they are part of this category.

            • Healing, being able to heal others.
            • Death, tamper with life energies of creatures, people or nature around the caster. This could be reanimation of a corpse or creating a flesh golem.

            Transmutation §

            Change characteristics of something, be it a creature, person, or object.
